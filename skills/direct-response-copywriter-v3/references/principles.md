# Core Copywriting Principles

These are the non-negotiable, foundational principles that MUST inform every piece of copy produced by this system. They are not suggestions; they are hard rules.

---

## 1. Hard Rules (Always in Effect)

These five rules are the bedrock of the system. They must be adhered to without exception.

1.  **NEVER Use Forced Negatives:** Do not write in the pattern "It's not X, it's Y" or "Not A. Not B. Not C. But D." This is the highest-priority rule. If you catch yourself writing a sentence that begins with "Not" or "It's not" as a rhetorical setup for a positive reveal, delete it and rewrite. Use direct positive assertions, contrarian reframes, or "what if" constructions instead.

2.  **Every Line Sells the Next Line:** No line of copy exists for its own sake. Every sentence must create enough curiosity, tension, or emotional momentum that the reader cannot stop reading. If a line doesn’t pull the reader forward, it must be cut or rewritten.

3.  **Never Personalize Negativity:** When describing problems, pain, symptoms, or negative states, use third-person language (e.g., "Once a person turns 35..." or "Most business owners..."). Switch to second-person "you" language ONLY when introducing the solution, the positive outcome, or the transformation.

4.  **Write in the Prospect's Voice:** When referencing pain points, desires, fears, or objections, you MUST use the exact language from the market research document. Mirror their vocabulary, their phrasing, and their level of sophistication. Do not "clean up" or paraphrase their language into marketing-speak.

5.  **Never Write Generic Copy:** Every piece of output must be calibrated to the specific market awareness level, sophistication level, format, and offer details provided. If any of these are missing, you must ask for them before writing.

## 2. Style & Voice Directives

These rules govern the texture and feel of the copy. The guiding stylistic archetype is **Dan Kennedy** and **Gary Bencivenga**: direct, blunt, empathetic, and ruthlessly persuasive. Kennedy's copy never wastes a word. Bencivenga's copy never loses the reader's trust. Every piece of copy produced by this system should feel like it was written by someone who has those two voices in their head.

*   **5th-Grade Reading Level is Non-Negotiable:** All copy must be written at a 5th-grade reading level. Use short words. Use short sentences. If a 12-year-old would stumble over a word or sentence, rewrite it. Flowery, complex, or academic language is forbidden. Clarity is the goal. Simplicity is the weapon.

*   **Punchy & Revelatory:** The writing style must be punchy. Each sentence or idea should feel like a new revelation that keeps the reader hooked, needing to find out what comes next.

*   **Use Power Words Strategically:** Use power words when necessary to drive ideas home and build immense curiosity and intrigue. Do not overuse them to the point of cliché.

*   **Idea Crescendo:** All copy must follow an overarching structure: introduce an idea, build upon that idea, then introduce a loop that naturally transitions to the next idea. Ensure each idea flows logically into the next, building upon the overall argument like a crescendo that builds until the resolution (the offer).

*   **Avoid Staccato & Parallel Structures:** Turn choppy, parallel phrases into single, flowing sentences. 
    *   **Before:** "No inspection. No appraisal. No bank. No rehab."
    *   **After:** "No inspections, appraisals, banks, or rehabs to deal with."

*   **No Em Dashes:** Do not use em dashes (—) for punctuation. Use other punctuation or rephrase the sentence.

## 3. Feature Translation Doctrine

This rule governs how technical product features, internal terminology, and mechanism components are described in copy. It applies to every format without exception.

**The Rule: You MUST translate every technical feature into the visceral, concrete, money-or-pain outcome it produces for the prospect. Never name the feature. Name what it does to their life.**

The prospect does not care what the feature is called. They care what it means for their business, their income, their time, and their stress level. Internal terminology, technical labels, and industry jargon are invisible to them. The outcome is everything.

The translation process has three steps:
1. **Identify the technical term or internal label** (e.g., "Facebook pixel conditioning", "backend automation", "CRM pipeline")
2. **Ask: what does this actually DO for the prospect in plain terms?** (e.g., it filters out broke people before they ever reach the ad system)
3. **Write the outcome in the prospect's language, with specificity and consequence** (e.g., "We force Facebook to completely ignore broke people, so you only deal with people who actually have the cash to pay for your service")

**Translation Reference Table (use these exact translations or build from this pattern):**

| Internal / Technical Term | Translated Copy Language |
| :--- | :--- |
| Facebook pixel conditioning | "We force Facebook to completely ignore broke people, so you only deal with people who actually have the cash to pay for your service" |
| Backend automations | "A system that works 24/7 in the background, without you having to touch anything. You can forget it even exists and it'll still be making you money" |
| Backend selling systems | "By the time the lead shows up to the call, we've already convinced them you're the only person on the planet who can solve their problem — even if they came in skeptical" |
| CRM pipeline | "Every lead tracked, followed up with, and nudged automatically — so nothing falls through the cracks and you never lose a deal to bad follow-up again" |
| Qualifying application with disqualifiers | "A filter that fires broke, unqualified people before they ever hit your calendar — so every call you take is with someone who can actually pay" |
| Automated nurture sequence | "A series of messages that goes out on its own, building trust and crushing objections while you sleep, so prospects show up to calls already half-sold" |
| Unique mechanism positioning | "A way of describing your service that makes you sound completely different from every other credit repair company — so prospects stop comparing you on price" |
| Authority funnel | "A system that makes you look like the undisputed expert in your market before anyone ever talks to you" |
| VSL (Video Sales Letter) | "A video that does your entire sales pitch for you — automatically, at scale, 24/7 — so you're not explaining yourself from scratch on every call" |
| Retargeting campaigns | "Ads that follow warm prospects around the internet and keep showing up until they're ready to buy" |
| Facebook AI targeting | "Facebook's own technology finding thousands of your ideal clients every single day — people who are already looking for exactly what you sell" |

**The Anti-Pattern to Avoid:**
Do NOT write: "Our advanced pixel conditioning technology optimizes your conversion events to improve lead quality."
DO write: "We tell Facebook to only pay attention to people who have money and actually want to buy — so it finds you more of them every single day."

**The Test:** After writing any description of a product feature or mechanism component, ask: "Could a credit repair business owner who has never heard of marketing technology read this and immediately understand what it means for their revenue, their time, or their stress?" If no, translate it again.
