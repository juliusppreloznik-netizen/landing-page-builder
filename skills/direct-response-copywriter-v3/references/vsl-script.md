# VSL Script Copywriting Skill

This skill provides the framework for creating long-form Video Sales Letter (VSL) scripts. VSLs are designed to take a prospect from being problem-aware to product-aware and ready to buy, all within a single video.

---

## 0. Declarative Knowledge Doctrine

When a VSL includes educational or explanatory content — such as explaining why the problem exists, how the mechanism works, or what makes the solution different — that content MUST be declarative, not procedural.

**Declarative knowledge teaches ABOUT something. It never teaches HOW to do something.**

Tell the prospect what the mechanism is, how it works conceptually, what outcomes it produces, and what it makes possible. Do not give them implementation steps. The moment you teach them how to do it, you eliminate the need to buy. The gap between understanding and implementation is what the product fills.

The test: after watching the VSL, could the prospect go implement the solution on their own without buying? If yes, you have given away too much. Pull back to the conceptual level.

## 2. VSL Script Framework

VSLs follow a narrative structure. The goal is to create and sustain a powerful emotional journey.

### Part 1: The Hook & Open Loop (First 1-2 Minutes)

-   **Open with a Strong Contrarian Hook:** Challenge a major assumption or use a pattern-interrupt to grab attention. For creative inspiration on hook formulas and promise stacks, consult the swipe file at `/home/ubuntu/skills/copywriter/references/swipe_file.md`. The following sections are worth drawing from — use the underlying idea or energy, not the exact words:
    *   **Section 1: Copy Payoff Template — "In the Next 7 Minutes" Promise Stack**
    *   **Section 8: VSL/TSL Opener — "Two Types of Coaches"**
    *   **Section 11: VSL Opener — "Five Jobs" Overwhelm Hook**
-   **Create an Open Loop:** Make a big promise or pose a question that the rest of the VSL will answer.

### Part 2: The Problem & Origin Story (Minutes 2-5)

-   **Agitate the Problem:** Use diagnostic framing to show you understand their struggle better than they do. Use their exact language from transcripts. (Reference: Playbook 1.4)
-   **Tell the Origin Story:** Transition into the story of how you (or a central character) faced the same problem, hit rock bottom, and went on a journey to find the solution. This builds rapport and credibility. (Reference: Playbook 3.1)

### Part 3: The "Aha!" Moment & Mechanism Reveal (Minutes 5-8)

-   **The Discovery:** Describe the moment of insight when the solution was discovered. This is the turning point of the story.
-   **Introduce the Unique Mechanism:** Give your proprietary system a name and explain how it works, connecting it to a Force of Nature. This is the logical justification for the results. (Reference: `unique_mechanism.md`)
-   **Apply Declarative Knowledge Here:** When explaining the mechanism, teach ABOUT it — what it is, how it works in principle, what it produces, what it makes possible. Do NOT walk the viewer through how to build or implement it themselves. The explanation should create understanding and desire, not a DIY roadmap.

### Part 4: Future Pacing & Desire Stacking (Minutes 8-10)

-   **Paint a Vivid Picture:** Describe the desirable future the prospect will experience once they have this solution. Use "Future Pacing" to make it feel inevitable. (Reference: Playbook 1.2)
-   **Stack the Desires:** Connect the tangible results of your product to the higher-order emotional benefits they truly want (freedom, status, peace of mind). (Reference: Playbook 1.3)

### Part 5: The Offer Stack & Close (Minutes 10-12+)

-   **Transition to the Offer:** Smoothly pivot from the story to the product you have created. ("Here's what I put together for you...")
-   **Stack the Offer:** List every component of the offer, assigning a high value to each one to build a massive perceived value.
-   **The Price Drop & Guarantee:** Reveal the total value, then reveal the much lower actual price. Follow with a strong risk-reversal guarantee.
-   **The Close:** Use a powerful closing framework like the "Two Futures" decision frame or the "Cost of Inaction" to compel a decision. (Reference: Playbook 4.2, 4.4)

## 3. Self-Revision Checklist

-   [ ] **Narrative Arc:** Does the VSL follow a clear story structure (struggle -> discovery -> solution)?
-   [ ] **Open Loop:** Is a compelling open loop created in the first 2 minutes and closed by the end?
-   [ ] **Emotional Journey:** Does the script intentionally move the prospect through a range of emotions (pain, hope, desire, urgency)?
-   [ ] **Mechanism Justification:** Is the Unique Mechanism presented as the logical and inevitable solution to the problem?
-   [ ] **Declarative Knowledge:** Does any educational or explanatory content in the VSL teach ABOUT the mechanism and solution conceptually, without giving away step-by-step implementation instructions?
-   [ ] **Value Stack:** Is the offer stack presented in a way that makes the price seem insignificant in comparison to the value?
-   [ ] **Clear CTA:** Is the call-to-action clear, direct, and compelling?
-   [ ] **Conversational Flow:** Does the script read naturally, with pauses and rhythm appropriate for a long-form video?
-   [ ] **No Forced Negatives:** Has the copy avoided the "It's not X, it's Y" pattern entirely? Zero instances allowed.
-   [ ] **Simplicity & Clarity:** Can anyone watch this VSL and immediately understand what is being offered and exactly why they should take action? If there is any moment of confusion, rewrite it.
-   [ ] **No Staccato Fragments:** Has the copy avoided a string of overly short, choppy sentences? Combine related ideas into single, flowing sentences. Go from "No skills needed. No experience. No special education." to "No skills, experience, or special education needed."
