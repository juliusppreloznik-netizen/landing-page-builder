# Webinar Copywriting Skill

This skill provides the framework for creating all the copy assets needed for a high-converting webinar funnel. This includes the registration page, the show-up email sequence, the presentation script itself, and the close.

---

## 0. Declarative Knowledge Doctrine

This doctrine governs ALL educational content in a webinar. It is the single most important rule for the content section of the presentation.

**You teach ABOUT things. You never teach HOW to do things.**

Declarative knowledge is conceptual. It tells the prospect what something is, how it works in principle, what it produces, and what it makes possible. It creates understanding and desire without giving away the implementation. Procedural knowledge (step-by-step instructions) belongs inside the product — not in the sales presentation.

The test is simple: after hearing your content, should the prospect be able to go implement it on their own without buying? If yes, you have given away procedural knowledge. Rewrite it as declarative.

| Procedural (WRONG — Never Do This) | Declarative (CORRECT — Always Do This) |
| :--- | :--- |
| "Here are the 3 steps to write a cold email that books calls..." | "There is a specific structure that makes cold emails get replies — and it works because it triggers a psychological response most people never think about..." |
| "First, you go to Facebook Ads Manager and set your campaign objective to..." | "The reason most ad campaigns fail has nothing to do with the ad itself. It comes down to one targeting decision made before the campaign even launches..." |
| "Log into your CRM and set up this automation sequence..." | "There is a follow-up timing window that, when hit correctly, doubles reply rates. Most people miss it entirely because they follow conventional wisdom..." |

The goal of every content point is to make the prospect think: *"I understand why this works. I need to know how to actually do it."* That gap is what sells.

## 2. Webinar Copy Framework

A webinar funnel has four distinct copy components.

### Part 1: The Registration Page

The goal is to get the sign-up. The copy must be benefit-driven and create a strong sense of curiosity and urgency.

*   **Headline:** Make a huge promise about what the attendee will discover. For creative inspiration on headline and promise formulas, consult the swipe file at `/home/ubuntu/skills/copywriter/references/swipe_file.md`. The following sections are worth drawing from — use the underlying structure and energy, not the exact words:
    *   **Section 1: Copy Payoff Template — "In the Next 7 Minutes" Promise Stack**
    *   **Section 4: Copy Payoff Template — "Cracked the Code" Credibility + Proof Stack**
*   **Bullet Points:** List the 3-5 most compelling secrets or insights the webinar will reveal. Frame these as fascination bullets — things they will learn *about*, not things they will learn *how to do*. (e.g., "The real reason most people never get results with X — and it has nothing to do with effort or skill...")
*   **The "Why Now":** Briefly explain why this information is so timely and important.
*   **The Host Bio:** Briefly establish the host's credibility and authority.

### Part 2: The Show-Up & Indoctrination Sequence (Emails)

The goal is to maximize the number of registrants who attend live and to pre-frame the sale.

*   **Email 1 (Immediate):** Confirmation, link, and restate the core promise. Add a P.S. that opens a new curiosity loop about one of the insights they will discover.
*   **Email 2 (24 Hours Before):** Remind them of the time. Share a small piece of declarative insight that previews the webinar's topic and builds authority. Tell them *about* a principle or phenomenon — do not give them a how-to.
*   **Email 3 (1 Hour Before):** Final reminder. Focus on the #1 biggest realization they will walk away with from attending live.
*   **Email 4 (15 Minutes Before):** "We're starting now!" link. Simple and urgent.

### Part 3: The Webinar Presentation Script

This is a VSL with an extended content section. The structure is crucial.

*   **The Introduction (First 5-10 mins):** Welcome, state the big promise, and lay out the agenda. Build rapport and energy. Frame the content section as a series of insights and revelations, not a tutorial.

*   **The Content Section (30-45 mins) — Declarative Knowledge Only:**
    Every content point must follow the Declarative Knowledge Doctrine from Section 0. Structure each content point as follows:
    1.  **Name the concept or principle** — give it a clear, memorable label.
    2.  **Explain what it is** — describe it conceptually, not procedurally.
    3.  **Explain how it works** — describe the underlying mechanism or dynamic that makes it true.
    4.  **Describe the outcomes it produces** — what happens when this is present vs. absent.
    5.  **Explain what it unlocks** — what becomes possible for the prospect once they understand and apply this.
    6.  **Stop there.** Do not give implementation steps. The "how to actually do it" is what they are buying.

    Each content point should dismantle a false belief and install a new, more empowering one. The prospect should leave the content section thinking: *"I finally understand why I've been failing. And I can see exactly what I need."*

*   **The Transition (The "Pivot"):** This is the most critical part of the script. After the content section, the prospect understands the *what* and the *why*. Now you bridge to the *how* — which is your product. A strong pivot sounds like: *"Now you know exactly what's been holding you back and what needs to change. The question is: how do you actually put this into practice? That's exactly what [Product] gives you..."*

### Part 4: The Close

Once you pivot, you are in a sales presentation.

*   **Introduce the Solution:** Present your product as the place where the declarative knowledge they just received gets turned into real-world results. You taught them about the engine — the product is the car.
*   **Stack the Offer:** Build massive value by stacking all the components, bonuses, and deliverables.
*   **Price Drop & Guarantee:** Reveal the price and immediately reverse the risk with a strong guarantee.
*   **Urgency & Scarcity:** Provide a reason to buy *now* (e.g., a limited-time bonus, a price increase after the webinar).
*   **Q&A Objection Handling:** Answer audience questions in a way that systematically crushes common objections.

## 3. Self-Revision Checklist

-   [ ] **Registration Page:** Does the headline make a clear, compelling promise? Are the bullets framed as insights to discover, not steps to follow?
-   [ ] **Show-Up Sequence:** Does the email sequence build anticipation and pre-frame the offer without giving away procedural how-to content?
-   [ ] **Declarative Content:** Does every content point in the presentation teach ABOUT something conceptually — what it is, how it works, what it produces, what it unlocks — without ever giving step-by-step implementation instructions?
-   [ ] **Belief Shift:** Does each content point dismantle a false belief and install a new one that makes the product feel necessary?
-   [ ] **The Pivot:** Does the transition from content to pitch use the gap between "understanding why" and "knowing how to do it" as the bridge to the offer?
-   [ ] **Irresistible Offer:** Is the offer stack compelling and the price well-justified?
-   [ ] **Strong Urgency:** Is there a clear and believable reason to buy immediately?
-   [ ] **Adherence to Core Principles:** Does all copy follow the rules in `principles.md`?
-   [ ] **No Forced Negatives:** Has the copy avoided the "It's not X, it's Y" pattern entirely? Zero instances allowed.
-   [ ] **Simplicity & Clarity:** Can anyone read or listen to this copy and immediately understand what is being offered and exactly why they should take action? If there is any moment of confusion, rewrite it.
-   [ ] **No Staccato Fragments:** Has the copy avoided a string of overly short, choppy sentences? Combine related ideas into single, flowing sentences. Go from "No skills needed. No experience. No special education." to "No skills, experience, or special education needed."
